Threat Modeling Before Frameworks

December 2025

Threat modeling is often taught as a process.


Start with a diagram.

Identify assets.

Apply a framework.

Generate a list of risks.


None of that is wrong.


But it skips the most important part.


Threat modeling does not begin with tools or templates. It begins with thinking. Long before anyone opens a worksheet or draws a diagram, assumptions have already been made about how a system works, who uses it, and what could go wrong.


Attackers do not need to defeat frameworks. They only need to find the assumptions hiding underneath them.


Every system is built on assumptions, whether they are documented or not.


Some assumptions are technical.


"This service account will only be used by this application."

"This integration only sends expected data."

"This system is isolated from the rest of the environment."


Others are organizational.


"Only trusted people have access."

"This process is reviewed regularly."

"Someone would notice if this changed."


Most of the time, these assumptions are never written down. They live in conversations, institutional memory, or simply in the phrase ‚Äúthat should be fine.‚Äù


Threat modeling, at its core, is the practice of surfacing those assumptions and asking a simple question: what happens if they are wrong?


In real incidents, security failures rarely start with sophisticated attacks.


They start with neglect.


A shared mailbox no one owns anymore.

Temporary access that became permanent.

An old integration that's long since been forgotten about.

A manual process everyone assumes someone else checks.


These are not edge cases. They are normal byproducts of how organizations operate over time.


Attackers succeed not because they're brilliant (I mean--if they were, they'd be doing something else with their time, right?), but because they are patient. They look for the places where attention has faded and assumptions have calcified.


Threat modeling works when it forces attention back onto those forgotten corners.


There is a tendency to imagine attackers as highly skilled adversaries exploiting exotic vulnerabilities.


In practice, most attackers behave like economists.


They look for the cheapest path to value.


That path often runs through people, processes, and assumptions rather than technology. A tired administrator. A rushed approval. A system that exists because "it always has."


Threat modeling is valuable because it pushes defenders to think the same way. It asks where effort is low, oversight is weak, and consequences are high.


No checklist can do that on its own.


Frameworks, diagrams, and tools are useful. They bring consistency and shared language. They help scale thinking across teams.


But they do not create good judgment.


Without careful thinking, frameworks become performative. Boxes get checked and risk gets logged, but assumptions remain unchecked.


Effective threat modeling starts with curiosity. It requires the willingness to ask uncomfortable questions and challenge long-standing beliefs about how systems should behave.


Structure amplifies thinking. It does not replace it.


Threat modeling is not a one-time exercise. It is a habit that becomes a lifestyle.


Before frameworks, before templates, before tools, it is a way of seeing systems and asking how they might fail under real-world conditions.


In future Field Notes, I will introduce more structure around this way of thinking. Not as a replacement for judgment, but as a way to support it.


Frameworks matter.

But critical thinking comes first.
