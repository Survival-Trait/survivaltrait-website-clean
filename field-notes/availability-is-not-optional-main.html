January 2026

Availability Is Not Optional


In cybersecurity, we often teach that security rests on three foundational pillars: confidentiality, integrity, and availability. All three matter. Neglecting any one of them weakens the system.


We usually talk about availability in terms of uptime, redundancy, and resilience. But availability has another, less discussed dimension: access to the knowledge required to operate systems securely.


When security knowledge is available only to those who can afford it, availability has already failed. Systems built devoid of security knowledge are not secure.


Cyber incidents continue to increase, and human error plays a role in most breaches. That reality changes the nature of the problem. Security is no longer something that belongs only to a dedicated security team--it belongs to everyone who designs systems, makes decisions, and uses technology as part of their daily work.


Over more than 25 years working in IT and cybersecurity across private industry, government, and critical infrastructure, I have seen the same pattern repeat: when incidents occur, it is rarely because a specific technology was missing. More often, priorities were unclear, ownership was ambiguous, risk was poorly understood, and critical knowledge never reached the people who needed it.


The failure was not technical. It was informational.


For a long time, I accepted this as part of the profession. Security knowledge is specialized. Training is expensive. Experience takes years to build. Some level of gating felt inevitable. Eventually, that assumption stopped making sense.


The knowledge I've accumulated through decades of hands-on work, teaching, mentoring, and learning from failures should not be locked behind a paywall. Restricting access to cybersecurity knowledge does not make systems safer. It makes the entire ecosystem more fragile.


We do not treat other forms of safety this way. Basic fire safety guidance is widely available. Public health information is shared openly. When risks become systemic, access to knowledge becomes a public necessity.


Cybersecurity has reached that point.


Digital systems now underpin financial markets, healthcare, energy, transportation, and communication. When people don't understand the risks they are interacting with, the consequences extend well beyond a single organization. Those failures cascade outward to customers, partners, and communities.


Access to security knowledge does not eliminate risk. But it does improve decision-making.


Informed people ask better questions. They recognize assumptions. They understand tradeoffs. They are less likely to outsource responsibility to tools, vendors, or checklists.


That is why I am choosing to share what I know publicly.


Through these field notes, I will document lessons learned from real-world work, teaching, mentoring, and ongoing research. I will write about threat modeling, risk, decision-making, and the mindset required to think clearly about security in imperfect systems.


This is not about exposing secrets or criticizing organizations. It's about strengthening the ecosystem by making knowledge accessible to anyone who wants to learn.


Because resilient systems do not start with better tools.


They start with informed people.
